## My Reflection

Before starting this research, I thought career planning in software engineering was mostly about picking the right programming language or framework to learn. After going through articles, surveys, and real developer discussions, I realize that this is a much deeper challenge and honestly, more interesting than I expected.

**What surprised me most** was how strongly experienced developers emphasize fundamentals over tools. I expected the advice to be "learn the latest AI tools." Instead, again and again, the message was: understand how things work at a deep level, because tools come and go. An engineer who truly understands systems can pick up any new tool. An engineer who only knows tools is replaceable the moment those tools change. This shifted how I think about what to focus on in my studies right now.

**What concerns me** is the pace of change. Reading the Stack Overflow survey and seeing that over 76% of developers are already using AI tools made the situation feel very real and urgent. It is easy to think "I'll catch up later," but the industry is not waiting. At the same time, I am concerned about a different risk: engineers who rely so heavily on AI generated code that they stop deeply understanding what they ship. If I graduate without truly understanding what I'm building, I will be in a fragile position professionally.

**The opportunity I see** is that this moment is actually a good time to build a strong reputation. If most engineers are rushing to use AI surface level, engineers who combine AI fluency with real depth will stand out. The bar for standing out is not as high as it seems — it just requires being deliberate when others are not.

**Which skills I think will remain important** after this research, I believe system design, critical thinking, debugging, and communication will remain central. AI can generate code, but someone still needs to decide what to build, catch the errors AI makes, and communicate with clients and teams. These are fundamentally human skills.

**How I will personally adapt** I am going to be more intentional about my GitHub activity. Before this assignment, I treated it as a place to upload university projects. Now I understand it is essentially my professional portfolio. I also want to start contributing to at least one open-source project before I graduate, even in a small way, because the experience of working in a real large codebase is something I cannot get from class projects alone.

**Did my opinion change?** Yes. I was slightly anxious about AI before this research — worried it might make software engineering as a career less stable. After the research, I feel more confident. The engineers who approach AI thoughtfully, who keep learning, and who build real things will have stronger careers, not weaker ones. The risk is not AI — the risk is complacency.
